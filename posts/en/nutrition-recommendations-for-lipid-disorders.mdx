## What is a lipid disorder?

A lipid disorder, also called dyslipidemia, means that blood lipid levels are outside the healthy range. This often involves elevated LDL cholesterol, triglycerides or other lipid markers. These values can be measured with a blood test.

## Why lipid disorders matter

Elevated lipid levels can increase the risk of atherosclerosis, heart attack and stroke. Because lipid disorders often cause no symptoms, regular check-ups and early diagnosis are important.

## Common causes

Some lipid disorders are genetic. Others are influenced by nutrition, body weight, lack of movement, diabetes, thyroid disorders, liver disease, medication, alcohol or other health conditions.

## Nutrition as part of treatment

Lifestyle changes are often the first step. Medication may also be necessary depending on individual risk, values and medical history.

### Prefer plant-focused meals

Vegetables, fruit, whole grains and legumes provide fiber and can help lower LDL cholesterol as part of a balanced diet.

### Include nuts

A daily portion of nuts can support a heart-healthy diet because they contain unsaturated fats and fiber.

### Choose healthy fats

Use olive oil, nuts, seeds and avocado more often, and reduce saturated fats from fatty meat, butter and high-fat dairy products.

### Reduce highly processed foods

Processed foods often contain excess sugar, salt and unhealthy fats. Reducing them supports lipid metabolism and body weight.

## Medical follow-up

Nutrition is only one part of care. Lipid values should be interpreted together with blood pressure, diabetes risk, family history, smoking status and other cardiovascular risk factors.
